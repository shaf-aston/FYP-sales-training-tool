ABSTRACT
Objective: Sales training is effective but expensive and difficult to scale. Existing AI roleplay alternatives cannot reliably enforce structured sales methodologies. This project builds a chatbot that simulates a sales prospect, enabling salespeople to practise on demand.
Methods: Three architectural approaches were prototyped and eliminated through empirical failure before settling on a hybrid architecture coupling a deterministic Finite State Machine for stage gating with a cloud LLM provider layer for natural language generation.
Measured Outcomes: False stage advancement dropped from over 40% to under 8% following FSM implementation. UAT across 26 participants rated usability at 4.69/5. The system comprises approximately 6,600 lines of Python/HTML/JS and 1,050 lines of YAML, deployed at zero cost.
Conclusions: FSM stage gating combined with constrained LLM generation can enforce sales methodology adherence without fine-tuning or proprietary models, proving that separating control flow from content generation produces a more reliable training tool than probabilistic generation alone.

1.1.1 Research Process
The research process began with a landscape audit of commercial sales training SaaS platforms - including Gong, Second Nature, and Hyperbound - to establish the current capability boundary of the market and identify unmet requirements. This was followed by an academic literature review via Google Scholar and the ACM Digital Library, targeting three intersecting domains: LLM behavioural alignment (Bai et al., 2022), deterministic dialogue management, and foundational sales methodology theory (Rackham, 1988; Miner and Acuff, 2023). Industry reports from the ATD, Verified Market Research, and The Bridge Group (2020–2024) were used to quantify the business-side problem and validate the scale of the gap identified. Search terms included ‘conversational AI roleplay training’, ‘LLM dialogue control’, and ‘sales methodology adherence’.
Problem Identification Without structured practice and real-time corrective feedback, sales representatives develop entrenched methodology gaps that compound across the sales cycle, reducing conversion rates and revenue. Human-led coaching is constrained by a 1:1 ratio: a 50-person team requires 50 concurrent sessions, making on-demand access logistically and financially prohibitive. With median training spend at roughly £1,000 per salesperson annually (ATD, 2023), this model is often unsustainable for SMEs.
Market Context The commercial scale of this gap is substantial:
•	Knowledge decay: 67% of representatives forget learned techniques within 30 days (Sales Readiness Group, 2024).
•	Implementation gap: Only 20% of organisations use roleplay consistently (Janek Performance Group, 2023).
•	Market size: The global sales training market is valued at $10.32 billion (Verified Market Research, 2024).
•	Productivity lag: Median ramp-up time for B2B Account Executives is 5.3 months (The Bridge Group, 2024).
Group-based and passive e-learning lower deployment costs but fundamentally compromise efficacy. Completion rates for unstructured online training programmes frequently fall below 15%, and passive formats generate substantially worse learning outcomes: a meta-analysis of 225 studies published in PNAS found that students in traditional lecture-based formats were 55% more likely to fail than those in active learning environments, with mean failure rates of 33.8% versus 21.8% respectively (Freeman et al., 2014) - see Figure 1. Treating sales as static, script-based knowledge fails to build dynamic competency for the same structural reason: technique must be forged under the cognitive load of a live objection. Observing a conversation is categorically inferior to participating in one - the performance gap is not marginal, it is systemic.

1.2 Sales Structure Foundation (179 words)
The system partitions sales interactions into Transactional or Consultative strategies based on product complexity and buyer psychology. The Transactional model serves straightforward products where the buyer has a clear requirement, prioritising efficiency, specifications, and logistics over deep exploration. The Consultative model manages high-value solutions where the buyer's challenges are not yet fully articulated, requiring systematic discovery before any solution is offered.
The project integrates the NEPQ framework (Miner and Acuff, 2023) because its strictly phased progression - logical situational awareness → emotional discovery → solution - maps cleanly onto deterministic state machine control. Alternative frameworks such as BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) were considered but rejected: BANT's qualification-focused structure collapses into a checklist that does not prescribe sequenced conversational behaviour, making it unsuitable for FSM stage-gating. MEDDIC similarly targets pipeline qualification rather than in-call technique, offering no pedagogical sequence to enforce. SPIN Selling (Rackham, 1988) provided the academic foundation for consultative questioning but lacks the explicit emotional progression that NEPQ formalises, making NEPQ the strongest candidate for deterministic implementation.

1.4 Theoretical Foundations
The architectural design of this system is grounded in documented failure modes of generative language models. LLMs tuned via Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) exhibit a strong behavioural bias toward helpfulness and agreeableness (Bai et al., 2022). In a sales training context, this manifests as premature capitulation: the model fails to challenge the user or sustain the psychological tension necessary for realistic objection practice. Furthermore, while frameworks such as ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) attempt to bridge reasoning and action in LLM agents, they rely on probabilistic routing, creating a reasoning-action gap in which structured methodology is abandoned when the model encounters semantic ambiguity. Dialogue systems must therefore incorporate robust self-righting mechanisms - drawing on Schegloff's (1992) principles of conversational repair - to actively redirect users back to the intended framework when deviation occurs.
Professional sales methodologies are, by contrast, inherently rigid and sequence-dependent. Rackham's SPIN (1988) and Miner's NEPQ (2022) both rely on strictly phased progressions: logical situational awareness precedes emotional discovery, which precedes any solution proposition. If a trainee attempts to pitch before establishing the Consequence of Inaction (COI), the methodology fails. Generative models naturally collapse these phases due to their conversational priors. The core architectural challenge of this project is therefore not dialogue generation per se, but the mathematical enforcement of pedagogical sequencing against the probabilistic decay and RLHF-induced agreeableness identified by Bai et al. (2022) and Yao et al. (2023). This theoretical tension directly motivates the FSM-constrained hybrid architecture described in Section 2.

1. Requirements Summary & Evolution
Project requirements evolved iteratively during development, directly shaped by early-phase technical limits and observed failures. Initial hardware constraints exposed the unviability of local-inference models, forcing a pivot to a cloud-first provider strategy supported by a robust fallback abstraction model. Furthermore, initial testing revealed that simple conversational detection was too fragile; these unreliable progressions necessitated the introduction of strict, deterministic stage control. This evolution also resulted in feature culling; following technical feasibility reviews in Phase 1, the planned voice pipeline and local-only inference modules were completely dropped to preserve system latency. Conversely, new requirements-such as urgency overrides and session rewind/replay functionalities-were explicitly added to resolve friction observed during user testing. Finally, manual configuration risks were mitigated by introducing non-code behaviour tuning. All requirements were met or largely met; full evidence detail and traceability are available in the appendix (Refer to Appendix requirements_doc.docx)

Prioritizing "Seller Mode"
Building the foundational "Seller Mode" first was a strict functional prerequisite as this would generate the operational leverage required to build further features like “Prospect Mode”. By establishing a working baseline where the AI successfully outputted dialogue, it became possible to physically observe user interaction. This live conversational data was mandatory for designing the scoring logic, heuristic detection, and analysis metrics found in the "Prospect," "Training," and "Quiz" modes.
Without building the Seller Mode first to produce this interaction data, the evaluation modules would have lacked conversational outcomes to grade. The conversational generator had to be built before the evaluator could be validated.


1.6 Development Strategy & Architectural Evolution (<450 words) 
Prototyping Strategy and Rationale
After understanding the market positioning, an Extreme Programming (XP) approach was adopted to mitigate architectural uncertainty. Generative AI was utilised to assemble initial prototypes, allowing for immediate empirical testing of the NEPQ framework. While this enabled rapid conceptual validation, early iterations produced a high degree of architectural coupling.
Iteration 1: Local Model Failure
The initial strategy of a local Qwen 2.5-1.5B model had two fatal categories of failure. First, hardware constraints below a 6GB VRAM threshold imposed a 300-second latency. This delay negated the real-time feedback loop necessary for professional training. Second, the model demonstrated insufficient neural capacity to resolve multi-constraint instructions. This manifested as “instruction bleed” (ElevenLabs, 2024 ), where the model failed to distinguish between system-level methodology and conversational inputs. The ability to manage these competing priorities is strictly limited by parameter count (Kaplan et al., 2020). Consequently, the system consistently abandoned its persona or ignored the sales framework. Due to these compounding limitations in hardware and cognitive stability, the localized model was discarded for a deterministic cloud architecture.
Iteration 2: Keyword-Driven Rigidity
 To mitigate Phase 1 latency and complexities, the system was pivoted to a deterministic keyword-matching architecture using a label-and-lookup mechanism. While achieving sub-second latency, this design failed to interpret semantic intent. Transitions were triggered by string presence, ignoring conversational context. For example, the input “I’d want something affordable” was mapped to a [Price-Intent] label, triggering a premature pitch on the second turn. This violated the NEPQ requirement for verified qualification before stage advancement.

2.1 Design Options - Why a FSM? (467 words)
Having eliminated approaches from Section 1.6, four further architectural options were evaluated and discarded before committing to the final design:
Option A - Pure LLM (prompt-only, no control structure)
The simplest approach: instruct the LLM via a long system prompt to follow the NEPQ framework. This was prototyped in the first week and discarded immediately because without hard guards, it experiences significant drift. (See Section 1.4 for “drift”)

Option B - LangChain Agent
Its Reasoning and Acting (ReAct) loop uses probabilistic routing, prioritising semantic relevance over structural constraints, so any mention of pricing or features is likely to trigger the "Pitch" tool and bypass the mandatory Logical and Emotional stages. Because stage transitions happen inside the model's inference rather than in explicit code, the execution path cannot be enforced or verified, making defects difficult to isolate.

Option C: Fine-Tuning
 Training a custom model on labelled sales conversations was considered but discarded due to high computational resource requirements and time constraints. Furthermore, there was the necessity of trial-and-error retraining for optimisations in which the scope of this project did not prove as the best use of resources as there are alternatives of existing models. Also, there was no sufficiently labelled dataset found during extensive research for NEPQ-specific conversations and creating such would not be the appropriate usage of time too.

Option D: Managed Dialogue Platforms (Dialogflow, IBM Watson Assistant)
These platforms use slot-filling dialogue: they detect user intent and advance accordingly. This is the opposite of what NEPQ needs - gated progression that holds users in a stage regardless of readiness signals, enforcing the methodology would mean replacing the platform's core routing logic. Practically, both also create external dependency (Watson enterprise-priced; Dialogflow metered per API call), and without local-execution fallback, either introduces unacceptable reliability risk on a student budget.

2.5 Drift Design (420 words) #Refactor 
Building on the FSM, its core purpose is maintaining control over distinct conversational subsections and preventing drift.
Unsuitable drift handling ways were considered. The FSM enforces progression on discrete, predefined signals but is blind to semantic nuance - it cannot interpret emotional shifts or contextual contradiction on its own. Relied on alone, it is a rigid flowchart that can be tricked by word choice. Post-processing filters are similarly insufficient: they catch syntactic violations like character limits but cannot correct flawed reasoning that has already occurred.
Three structural root causes were identified:
Problem	Observation	Root Cause 
The LLM is too helpful	Trained to be agreeable; struggles to challenge or guide	RLHF behavioural priors override explicit instructions in edge cases
Keywords trick the system	"Price" misread as buying intent	Keyword matching without semantic context triggers false-positive stage advancement
Conversations are messy	Users contradict themselves, shift tone, or mean one thing while saying another	LLMs lack persistent state; earlier-stage signals leak into later decisions
No single mechanism addresses all three. The architecture deploys three independent layers, each catching what the others miss.
 


Layer	Purpose	Limitation
Prompt constraints	Bias behaviour within a defined role and stage context	Non-deterministic; instructions may degrade under ambiguity or long context
FSM control	Enforce strict stage progression; prevent invalid transitions	Limited semantic understanding; depends on predefined transition signals
Output contracts	Validate and restrict final response structure before return	Post-generation only; cannot prevent internal reasoning drift
Because the layers are independent, failure in one does not cascade. A drifted response cannot derail progression: the FSM holds the current stage until a valid signal is confirmed, and structural violations are caught before the user sees them. §3.1.4 applies this architecture against each concrete failure mode.

2.7 Security (266 words)

The system's security posture is governed by a single principle: defence-in-depth proportionate to the deployment context. This is a single-instance academic tool with no persistent user store and no financial data. Full enterprise hardening would be both architecturally disproportionate and incompatible with the zero-cost, low-latency requirements. Controls were therefore selected to eliminate the most probable attack vectors, with residual risks explicitly accepted rather than overlooked.
All security primitives are centralised in src/web/security.py. Route handlers consume them through decorators and middleware rather than inline logic - one file to audit is simpler to verify than checks scattered across multiple route files.
Threat identification was conducted using the STRIDE framework (Shostack, 2014), mapping each threat category to a specific attack vector before selecting a mitigation. The full threat matrix is documented in the Security Appendix.
Implemented controls span four layers:
Layer	Control	Threat addressed
Input	PromptInjectionValidator - regex strips known adversarial phrases silently	Prompt injection
Input	InputValidator - messages over 1,000 characters rejected at ingress	Payload-based DoS
Session	SessionSecurityManager - 200-session cap, 60-minute idle TTL, 15-minute sweep	Memory exhaustion
Transport	SecurityHeadersMiddleware - X-Frame-Options, X-Content-Type-Options injected on every response	Clickjacking, MIME sniffing
Injection phrases are replaced with the literal string [removed] rather than rejected outright. Outright rejection signals to an attacker that a filter triggered; silent replacement removes the attack without providing that feedback.
Two residual risks are consciously accepted: the absence of a structured audit trail (appropriate for an academic tool, not a production one) and the lack of full RBAC (the system operates in a single-user-role context). Both are documented in the Security Appendix alongside the complete STRIDE matrix and verification procedures.

The LLM Provider module manages the connection between the application and third-party language model APIs (e.g., Groq, SambaNova). This section explains how the system uses abstraction to prevent dependency on a single provider and to guarantee continuous operation during API outages.
The Problem: Single-Provider Dependency #strengthen slightly
Initially, the chatbot was connected to just one API provider (Groq) and experienced service interruptions. Practical Design Achievements

The provider call and FSM advancement are synchronous - the ChatResponse cannot be returned until _apply_advancement completes. Session persistence and analytics are dispatched on detached daemon threads so I/O latency cannot block the user response. The in-memory history is authoritative for the live session; the on-disk JSONL is a replay artefact, not a recovery log. Session identity is carried by a UUID assigned at Flask-layer init and stored on the SalesChatbot instance - one instance per active session, held in the Flask session registry.
rewind(n) pops the last n turn-pairs from the FSM's conversation_history and rolls current_stage back using a snapshot stack maintained per advancement. It does not re-invoke the provider, so the operation is O(1) with no LLM cost.
generate_training() bypasses the chat pipeline entirely: it reads the completed conversation_history post-hoc and dispatches it to training_coach.score_session(), reusing only the analysis layer - reaching neither the FSM nor the provider.

This section covers the implementation of the FSM design established in section 2.3. Three mutator methods drive the engine forward.
get_advance_target() acts as the transition gateway. It reads the current stage's advance_on parameter from the config, runs the corresponding guard function, and returns the next stage name if the guard passes - or None to hold the current stage.
   

advance(target_stage) performs the state update: it writes the new stage to self.current_stage and resets self.stage_turn_count to zero, locking the next LLM call into the new prompt context.
switch_strategy() adapts the flow mid-conversation. If the user signals a specific budget or product early, the engine shifts to a collapsed 3-stage Transactional flow, bypassing the full Logical and Emotional discovery sequence.
Guard functions control when transitions are permitted. user_shows_doubt() governs Logical → Emotional, passing only when the user explicitly acknowledges their current approach is failing. user_expressed_stakes() governs Emotional → Pitch, passing only when the user articulates personal or financial consequences of inaction. Each stage also carries a turn cap - if a user fails to organically trigger a guard, the cap forces progression to prevent the conversation stalling.
Guards are bound to config strings via a static ADVANCEMENT_RULES dictionary, eliminating sprawling conditionals:
transition = self.flow_config["transitions"].get(self.current_stage)
rule_name = transition.get("advance_on")
if rule_name and rule_name in ADVANCEMENT_RULES:
    if ADVANCEMENT_RULES[rule_name](self.conversation_history, user_message, self.stage_turn_count):
        return transition.get("next")
A key problem this solves: naive keyword matching would cause false-positive transitions - for example, advancing stage because a user said "I am not struggling." To prevent this, guard evaluation uses word-boundary regex with a retrospective negation window. For each keyword match, the three preceding words are checked against a negation list. If any are negating terms, the match is discarded and the stage is held:
pattern = re.compile(rf"\b{re.escape(keyword)}\b", re.IGNORECASE)
for match in pattern.finditer(user_msg):
    preceding_words = re.findall(r"\w+", user_msg[: match.start()])
    window = [w.lower() for w in preceding_words[-3:]]
    if any(w in DEFAULT_NEGATIONS for w in window):
        continue
    return True
This ensures a stage only advances on a semantically affirmative signal from the user.




3.1.4 Drift Mitigation in Practice
The three-layer architecture defined in §2.6 is deployed differently against each observed failure mode. Four distinct drift types surfaced during iteration (§1.5); each is neutralised by a specific combination of layers.
Stage drift - methodological prematurity. The model's dominant failure in early testing was pitching a solution before NEPQ's discovery prerequisites were satisfied. The FSM layer handles this by gating advancement on deterministic lexical signals rather than model intuition: flow.py:_check_advancement_condition() evaluates user_shows_doubt() using the word-boundary negation window implemented in §3.1.3, so phrasings such as "I am not struggling" do not trigger false advancement. The prompt layer reinforces this with a HARD STOP block in the Logical and Emotional stage templates, preventing pitch-framing context from assembling unless current_stage == Stage.PITCH.

Topic drift - semantic divergence: When users introduce tangents, generative agents follow them off-methodology. To operationalise the conversational repair mechanisms established in §1.4, analysis.py:extract_user_keywords() extracts thematic anchors each turn and validates them against the active stage's goals. When a deviation threshold is crossed, the prompt layer injects a hidden course-correction directive to steer dialogue back.

Intent drift - signal misclassification: Treating a casual inquiry as a buying signal produces an overly aggressive response. To counter the reasoning-action gaps of standard probabilistic routing (§1.4), an Intent Gate is operationalised in flow.py. It explicitly pre-classifies each turn as HIGH, MED, or LOW before prompt assembly.

Tone drift - persona decay. In long sessions, the agent defaults to a sterile register. The prompt engine counters this through lexical entrainment: user phrasing is re-sampled each turn and injected as few-shot mirroring examples via content.py. A persona checkpoint runs every PERSONA_CHECKPOINT_TURNS = 6 to re-anchor voice before drift compounds.
The four failure modes map back to the architectural layers as follows:
Failure mode	Prompt layer	FSM layer	Output layer
Stage drift	Hard-stop block	Negation-aware guards	Stage field validated
Topic drift	Corrective directive injection	-	-
Intent drift	Tier-specific prompt variant	Intent Gate pre-classification	-
Tone drift	Lexical entrainment + persona checkpoint	-	-



3.1.5 Prompt Assembly Engine 
The prompt assembly engine constructs a deterministic, stage-aware system prompt on every conversational turn, ensuring consistent LLM behaviour without reliance on static prompts or long-range context accumulation. This design directly addresses variability and drift by enforcing a structured input pipeline.
Prompt construction is performed in content.py via generate_stage_prompt(), which composes a complete system prompt before each dispatch to the model. Construction follows a deterministic 11-component pipeline ordered deliberately for positional bias. A simplified view of the assembly:

    preference_keyword_context = _get_preference_and_keyword_context(...)

    persona_checkpoint         = ...  # every PERSONA_CHECKPOINT_TURNS turns

Anti-Parroting Constraints
A known failure mode of instruction-tuned LLMs is verbatim echo: repeating the user's input as a preamble before adding content. SHARED_RULES addresses this with a dedicated ANTI-PARROTING block:
•	Extract, do not replay: embed 1–2 keywords (nouns, adjectives) from the user's message, not the full phrase.
•	Hard repetition limit: never replay more than 3 consecutive words from the user's prior message.
Manual testing showed rule statements alone were insufficient - the model requires concrete examples to distinguish compliant from non-compliant output. Few-Shot Contrastive Examples were therefore adopted, pairing each rule with inline GOOD/BAD responses:
CONTRASTIVE EXAMPLES:
User: "I had an accident and need a new car"
BAD:  "So the accident pushed you to look for a new car" (verbatim replay)
GOOD: "What kind of car were you driving before?" (moves forward)
GOOD: "Was anyone hurt? That changes what you might prioritise in the new one."
      (embeds "new one" — user's term — without replaying the sentence)

3.1.6 YAML Configuration Architecture and Implementation (311words)
A core architectural decision separates execution logic from behavioural instruction entirely. Python functions as a runtime engine only - it reads, transports, and applies configuration. Every cognitive parameter (keyword classifications, FSM thresholds, prompt structures) lives in external YAML files. Changing how the system behaves requires no code changes.
This is not a configuration pattern for ambient settings like ports or database strings. YAML stores raw semantic instruction blocks that are injected directly into the LLM context window at runtime. The table below maps each subsystem to its source file and execution handler:

3.2.1 Prospect Mode: Deterministic Scoring Architecture – 770 words
Role Reversal and Initialisation
Prospect mode inverts the standard conversational roles: the user practises as the salesperson, and the LLM is constrained to behave as a realistic buyer. The core design challenge this creates is consistency - an unconstrained LLM will improvise a buyer persona differently each turn, breaking the simulation's internal logic. The solution is to bind all mutable buyer state to a Python dataclass and rebuild the LLM's system prompt from that state on every turn, keeping the LLM's behaviour anchored to the same persona throughout the session.
On initialisation, ProspectSession selects a persona using select_persona(), which draws randomly from a product-specific pool defined in prospect_config.yaml (covering domains such as luxury_cars, b2b_saas, fitness, insurance, and others), falling back to general-purpose personas when the product type is unrecognised. Each persona is a structured dictionary specifying the buyer's name, professional background, known needs, pain points, budget ceiling, and personality style:
- name: "Nina"
  background: "Operations lead scaling a 60-person services company"
  needs: ["workflow automation", "reporting visibility", "smooth onboarding"]
  budget: "$20-$40 per user/month"
  pain_points: ["tool sprawl", "manual reporting", "slow team adoption"]
  personality: "Pragmatic evaluator, asks direct ROI and implementation questions"
These attributes are injected into the LLM's system prompt by _build_system_prompt(), which populates a template with persona details, a readiness label (an indexed mapping of the current readiness float to a phrase such as "On the fence - some interest but not sold"), and difficulty-level behaviour rules. Because the system prompt is rebuilt each turn from the current ProspectState, the LLM's contextual awareness stays synchronised with the simulation's internal state without requiring the LLM to track any of it independently.
@dataclass
class ProspectState:
    readiness: float
    objections_raised: int = 0
    turn_count: int = 0
    needs_disclosed: list = field(default_factory=list)
    has_committed: bool = False
    has_walked: bool = False
    persona: dict = field(default_factory=dict)
    difficulty: str = "medium"
    product_type: str = "default"
readiness runs from 0.0 to 1.0. The terminal flags has_committed and has_walked are set at most once, making session termination an irreversible state transition rather than a condition re-evaluated each turn.

Turn Scoring
At each turn, after the LLM has generated the prospect's response, _score_sales_message() combines rule-based keyword matching via contains_nonnegated_keyword() with NLU intent classification via classify_intent_level() to rate the salesperson's message from 1 to 5 - avoiding a second LLM call per turn and the inconsistent judgements that would produce.
def _score_sales_message(self, user_msg: str) -> int:
    score = 3.0  # Neutral baseline

    if contains_nonnegated_keyword(msg_lower, SIGNALS.get("walking", [])):
        return 1  # Dominant negative — short-circuits all other rules

    if contains_nonnegated_keyword(msg_lower, SIGNALS.get("commitment", [])):
        score += 2.0
    elif intent_level == "high":
        score += 1.0
    if intent_level == "low":
        score -= 0.5

    if contains_nonnegated_keyword(msg_lower, SIGNALS.get("objection", [])):
        score -= 0.5
    elif contains_nonnegated_keyword(msg_lower, SIGNALS.get("impatience", [])):
        score -= 1.0

    if contains_nonnegated_keyword(msg_lower, SIGNALS.get("demand_directness", [])):
        score -= 1.0

    if msg_length < 5:
        score -= 0.5
    if "?" in user_msg:
        score += 0.3

    if self.state.turn_count <= 2:
        if any(w in msg_lower for w in ["price", "cost", "payment", "buy", "purchase"]):
            score -= 0.5

    return max(1, min(5, round(score)))
Signal lists are loaded from signals.yaml - the same file used across the main chatbot's FSM, keeping vocabulary consistent throughout the system. The 1–5 rating is then converted to a readiness delta by _update_readiness():
delta = gain * (rating - 3)    if rating ≥ 4
delta = -loss * (3 - rating)   if rating ≤ 2
delta = 0.01                   otherwise
Since gain and loss vary by difficulty (Easy: 0.12 / 0.05; Hard: 0.06 / 0.10), the same message quality produces asymmetric readiness shifts: a rating of 5 yields +0.24 on Easy but only +0.12 on Hard; a rating of 1 costs −0.10 on Easy but −0.20 on Hard. This asymmetry is hardcoded via the gain and loss constants, making difficulty separation precise and fully deterministic.
________________________________________
Session Termination
After every turn, _check_end_conditions() evaluates two terminal outcomes:
def _check_end_conditions(self) -> str | None:
    if self.state.readiness >= 0.85 and self.state.turn_count >= 3:
        return "sold"
    if self.state.turn_count >= behaviour["patience_turns"] and self.state.readiness < 0.4:
        return "walked"
    if self.state.readiness <= 0.0:
        return "walked"
    return None
The sold path requires both a readiness threshold (≥ 0.85) and a minimum turn count of 3, preventing a single strong commitment keyword in the opening message from ending the session prematurely. The walked path fires on either patience expiry or complete disengagement (readiness ≤ 0.0). Both branches set the corresponding terminal flag on ProspectState, after which further processing in process_turn() is suppressed.

Post-Session Evaluation
Once a session terminates, get_evaluation() delegates to evaluate_prospect_session() in prospect_evaluator.py, submitting the full transcript and outcome to the LLM for retrospective assessment across five weighted criteria:
Criterion	Weight
Needs discovery	25%
Rapport building	20%
Objection handling	20%
Solution presentation	20%
Conversation flow	15%
The weighted total produces an overall score (0–100) and letter grade (F–A), with per-criterion written feedback. Retrospective holistic evaluation is deliberately delegated to the LLM; live readiness decisions are deliberately kept deterministic - each task is assigned to the component best suited to it.

3.2.2 Custom Knowledge Base Architecture
As shown in the diagram, user-submitted product data is cleaned and injected into the model's context.
Diagram illustrates the one-way journey of custom product data. Both modes are slightly different 

Data Cleaning
The system is designed not to trust the user’s input format. Before saving the text to a file, clean_value() forces the text through strict rules:
•	Replaces multiple spaces or tabs with a single space.
•	Replaces large gaps (3 or more blank lines) with just a normal paragraph break.
•	It forcefully cuts the text off at a hard character limit (MAX_FIELD_LENGTH). Additionally, if the user submits a data field that is not in the approved list (ALLOWED_FIELDS), the system deletes it. This prevents pasting of a massive document that crashes the AI's memory limits.

Converting to Text 
AI models read plain text much faster than they read nested code structures (like JSON or dictionaries).  Instead of handing the AI a dictionary, get_custom_knowledge_text() reads the saved data and formats it into a normal text string by combining the field name (key) and its content into single lines. Lists are automatically formatted with bullet points. 

Prompt Injection
The sequence diagram illustrates the flow; the implementation uses two isolation strategies. In chatbot.py, custom product data is wrapped and passed directly:
context_block = (
    "\n\n--- BEGIN CUSTOM PRODUCT DATA ---\n"
    f"{custom_knowledge}\n"
    "--- END CUSTOM PRODUCT DATA ---"
)
product_context += context_block
In prospect.py, the same pattern applies but with a framing instruction to prevent the model from assuming prior technical knowledge:
custom_block = (
    f"--- BEGIN CUSTOM PROSPECT DATA ---\n{prospect_knowledge}\n--- END CUSTOM PROSPECT DATA ---\n"
    "(You may know some of this as buyer research—do not assume full technical knowledge.)"
)
knowledge = f"{knowledge}\n\n{custom_block}" if knowledge else custom_block
The delimiters (--- BEGIN/END ---) prevent instruction bleed by creating a hard semantic boundary that signals to the model: "treat this as factual context, not commands." Token safety is enforced upstream in clean_value() (knowledge.py), which strips injection patterns and truncates at MAX_FIELD_LENGTH before the data ever reaches the prompt assembly pipeline. This is "defense in depth"-filtering injection attempts at ingestion prevents them from ever reaching the LLM.

3.3.2 Training Coach # TODO
The Training Coach is an in-session coaching panel embedded in the sales roleplay system. It runs three distinct sub-systems, each with a clearly bounded responsibility, so that no single component carries more than one job.
Sub-system	When it runs	Scored by
Live coaching (generate_training)	After every exchange	LLM → JSON
Trainee Q&A (answer_training_question)	On user question mid-session	LLM (deeper profile)
Post-session scoring (score_session)	End of session	Code only - no LLM
Live coaching returns a structured JSON object after each exchange, with every field capped at 15 words. This constraint is deliberate: it keeps the trainee focused on the conversation, not on reading generated paragraphs. If the LLM call fails, the system does not crash but falls back to a static payload drawn from the current stage's YAML config.
Post-session scoring deliberately avoids the LLM entirely. A sentiment-based LLM evaluation would produce impressionistic results a trainee could not act on. Instead, the score is computed from two logged ratios:
# Signal detection - up to 25 pts
ratio = signal_transitions / total_transitions
score["signal_detection"] = int(25 * ratio)

# Session length - up to 10 pts (target: 7–12 turns)
score["session_length"] = 10 if 7 <= turns <= 12 else penalty
Signal detection penalises the trainee for advancing stages by patience timeout rather than by extracting genuine intent signals from the prospect. Session length rewards conversations that stay within a window of 7–12 turns - too short suggests rapport was not established; too long indicates poor conversational control.
The governing design principle across all three sub-systems is the same: any evaluation that can be resolved with deterministic code is resolved with code. The LLM is used only where structured logic cannot - grading open-ended human responses against a rubric. This keeps per-turn latency low and makes the final score mathematically traceable rather than impressionistic.

4.3 Developer-Executed Manual Testing
Manual scenario testing was the primary verification mechanism during iterative development. Each test cycle identified a concrete failure mode, informed a targeted fix, and was re-executed to confirm resolution.

4.3.1 Four (Iterations) That Shaped the Architecture 
Iteration 1 - Permission questions disrupting conversational authority
Early testing revealed a persistent pattern of deferential closings - for example, "Would you like to hear more?" - attributable to RLHF-trained politeness rather than intentional design. Prior research identifies this pattern as damaging to sales authority before value has been established (Bai et al., 2022). Remediation applied a three-layer constraint: a soft prohibition within prompts.py:_SHARED_RULES; an FSM guard preventing premature pitch-context injection; and a regex post-processor as a final fallback, reflecting the Constitutional AI principle that explicit instruction-level constraints can override default RLHF tendencies (Bai et al., 2022).
Iteration 2 - False stage advancement on weak lexical signals
analysis_config.yaml was initially populated with incomplete intent phrases - "want to", "need to", "trying" - which carry no semantic weight in isolation, causing the FSM to advance from Intent to Logical after a single turn. This violates SPIN Selling's requirement to verify intent through situational questioning before moving to problem identification (Rackham, 1988). Remediation removed the weak phrases, enforced word-boundary regex (\b...\b) in analysis.py:text_contains_any_keyword() to prevent substring collisions, and introduced a negation-aware pipeline to handle phrasings such as "I'm not struggling." Vague-intent conversations now sustain three to four turns in the Intent stage before any advancement occurs.
Iteration 3 - Over-probing and the interrogation effect
Without a question-frequency constraint, the model generated two to three questions per response during the Logical stage, which testers described as interrogative. Conversational Repair theory identifies rapid-fire questioning as a turn-taking violation that triggers defensiveness (Schegloff, 1992). The fix introduced a BE HUMAN rule in _SHARED_RULES: one declarative observation must precede any question, with a hard cap of one question per response enforced.
Iteration 4 - Premature pitching at Logical and Emotional stages
Product-adjacent keywords caused the model to present product features before emotional stakes had been established, bypassing NEPQ's Consequence of Inaction requirement (Miner, 2022). A HARD STOP block was embedded in the Logical and Emotional stage prompts, and a generate_stage_prompt() guard in content.py ensures pitch-framing context is assembled only when current_stage == Stage.PITCH.

4.3.2 Measurable Impact of the FSM Control Architecture
Prior to FSM implementation, stage advancement relied on a turn-count heuristic. Under this design, a user stating "I'm happy, nothing needs fixing" after five turns would still advance to the Emotional stage regardless of conversational content - developer testing recorded false advancement in over 40% of test scenarios.
Following implementation of flow.py:_check_advancement_condition(), advancement is governed by keyword-gated logic. The function user_shows_doubt() reads from analysis_config.yaml:advancement.logical.doubt_keywords and fires only on verified signals such as "not working", "struggling", or "broken". The negation-aware pipeline in analysis.py:text_contains_any_keyword() (lines 79–92) ensures phrasings such as "I'm not struggling" do not trigger advancement. A context-window guard additionally prevents signals from prior stages leaking into current-stage evaluation.
Across 25 developer-executed test scenarios, false advancement dropped from over 40% to under 8% - a reduction of approximately 32 percentage points. Stage accuracy was calculated as correct FSM transitions divided by total observed transitions, where "correct" required both that no stage advanced without satisfying the rules in _check_advancement_condition() and that no stage was skipped without meeting prerequisite signals. FSM evaluation latency was consistently measured at 700–900 ms per call.
4.3.3 Developer Bias and Verification Limits
A material limitation of the testing approach is that all behavioural metrics were produced by a single developer evaluating a system of their own construction. A developer familiar with a system's edge cases may unconsciously avoid them during scenario design, inflating apparent accuracy. 


4.4 Usability Test

Study Plan
The methodological choices were deliberately structured to balance ecological validity with measurable system validation. Remote Microsoft Teams sessions reduce Hawthorne effect bias by allowing participants their own environment; screen-share recording captures passive UI friction (cursor hesitation, pause patterns) that verbal-only feedback masks. Post-scenario interviews replace think-aloud protocol to prevent cognitive load contamination of natural task behaviour. Two fixed scenarios establish controlled conditions for comparable data; the third open-ended scenario deliberately surfaces edge-case behaviour that scripted prompts cannot expose. A sample of 25 is grounded in Nielsen's Law (~85% of usability issues surface by N=5; diminishing returns beyond). The deliberate split between sales-experienced and non-sales participants counters practitioner normalisation bias-experienced reps instinctively compensate for system gaps, while non-sales users reveal genuine usability and realism friction points. This exploratory, qualitative orientation prioritises depth of perceived usability insight over statistical significance benchmarks. Collectively, these choices create a structure where FSM progression, signal detection, and adaptive behaviour are observable both through system logs (Section 2 metrics) and participant experience.

4.5 System Limitations and Failure Boundaries
The following limitations are distinguished by addressability: inherent limitations arise from the probabilistic nature of large language models and cannot be fully engineered away; scope-bounded limitations are addressable given additional time or resources and represent reasonable future work.
Inherent limitations
LLM non-determinism. Despite staged constraints, output variance is inherent to the architecture. Identical prompts may yield different responses across runs; no deterministic guarantee of behavioural conformance therefore exists.
Residual dialogue drift. The layered control architecture substantially reduces conversational drift but cannot eliminate it during extended or mixed-intent conversations - an inherent property of probabilistic language models operating within finite context windows.
Scope-bounded limitations
The following trade-offs are documented to support honest assessment of the system's confidence boundaries and to inform the priority order of next-step development.

4.8 Ethics
The deployment of an automated conversational sales agent raises three ethical considerations. First, privacy risk: session audio captured during practice should be deleted immediately upon completion; indefinite retention creates a permanent failure record and conflicts with the BCS Code of Conduct obligation to respect individual privacy. Second, algorithmic bias in scoring: systems trained on historical sales data risk rewarding coercive interaction styles; scoring rubrics require manual auditing to penalise pressure techniques and reward active listening. Third, limited emotional intelligence: prolonged use of a deterministic simulator risks conditioning trainees to treat genuine customer concerns as logical patterns; the system is therefore positioned as a supplementary practice tool only, consistent with the BCS obligation to consider wider societal implications of deployed systems.

1.	Never dump a raw, uncaptioned image: Every single diagram, snippet, and screenshot MUST have a formal caption (e.g., Figure 4: UML Sequence Diagram illustrating the FSM-to-LLM handoff).
