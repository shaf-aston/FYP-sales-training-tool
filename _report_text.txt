Contextual investigation & background research 1.1 Problem Domain & Business Context1.1.1 Problem IdentificationWithout practice and instant feedback, sales representatives may develop unidentified bad habits that compound over time, negatively impacting a company's revenue. Human–led coaching is constrained by a 1:1 ratio: a 50–person team requires 50 concurrent sessions, making on–demand access logistically and financially prohibitive. This model is often unsustainable for SMEs, particularly given the well–documented limitations of passive, technology–delivered training as a standalone solution (Welsh et al., 2003).The commercial scale of this gap is substantial:Knowledge decay and skill retention: Research on the science of training demonstrates that without reinforcement and active practice following initial exposure, skills deteriorate rapidly. Training must be treated as an ongoing system rather than a discrete event, as what occurs after training is as critical as the training itself (Salas et al., 2012; Ebbinghaus, 1885; Murre and Dros, 2015).Practice gap: Despite widespread recognition of the need for skill reinforcement, organisations consistently struggle to implement practice–based, application–focused training. Without structured needs assessment and evaluation at every stage, training failures and performance gaps go undetected (Attia et al., 2005).Investment inefficiency: Significant challenges remain in the content development, delivery and evaluation of sales training programmes. The intensified emphasis on accountability and rapidly changing sales roles makes it increasingly difficult for organisations to justify investment in training that lacks measurable, practice–based outcomes (Lassk et al., 2012). While passive e–learning is cost–effective to deploy, it fundamentally compromises results with even completion rates below 15%. Crucially, passive formats severely degrade learning outcomes. A landmark meta–analysis of 225 studies found that learners in traditional, lecture–based environments were 55% more likely to fail than those engaged in active learning (Freeman et al., 2014, see figure below). Sales techniques must be honed under the cognitive load of a live objection. Simply observing a conversation is categorically different from actively participating in one.Figure 1: The empirical case for active practice. Passive, observation–based learning (orange) consistently drives higher failure rates compared to active participation (blue). Source: Freeman et al. (2014).1.1.2 StakeholdersThis project presents a solution targeting three primary stakeholder cohorts:Sales Representatives require frequent application. Objection–handling degrades without low–stakes repetition against varied prospects, objections and conversational stages. An on–demand, non–judgemental roleplay system addresses this directly, building skill through volume independent of manager availability.Sales Managers are capped by availability for practise and feedback frequency. During roleplays, they may not accurately portray the range of prospect personalities that the product or service demands.L&D (Marketing Strategists) require sales systems have appropriate adherence to brand and methodology consistency across various team–sizes to leverage large, reproducible performance data for informed decision–making.1.2 Business Analysis & Competitive Differentiation The academic literature on conversational AI training is dominated by healthcare and customer service dialogue contexts, leaving sales methodology enforcement as a largely unexplored domain. Existing commercial solutions fall into three distinct categories: passive Learning Management Systems (e.g., Litmos, TalentLMS), retrospective call intelligence platforms (e.g., Gong, Chorus) and generative AI roleplay tools (e.g., Hyperbound, Second Nature).Competitive analysis based on published product documentationTable 1 reveals a consistent gap: no existing free or accessible solution combines active practice, live in–session feedback and deterministic methodology enforcement simultaneously. Generative competitors, such as Hyperbound, achieves some conversational authenticity through LLM generation, but this same mechanism prevents firm framework adherence. Manager–led roleplay achieves deterministic enforcement but is unscalable by definition. 1.3 Theoretical FoundationsThe architectural design of this system must defend against root causes of “LLM drift” – LLMs’ diverging tendency to ignore custom instructions in favour of its original training over the course of a conversation.First, models tuned via RLHF are trained to be highly agreeable and focused on overly user–pleasing behaviour (Bai et al., 2022). Although advantageous in some contexts, in a sales training context this is fatal; shown as premature collapse – is convinced too easily, instead of pushing back where needed. Hence, the model fails to sustain psychological resistance where necessary for realistic objection practice. Secondly, even when reasoning steps are made explicit, like in agentic frameworks such as ReAct (Yao et al., 2023), the choice of which action to take next is still drawn from the model's prior training, not from the prompt – here termed as the “instruction–behaviour gap”. The prompt is simply just more tokens in the context window, competing for influence against the patterns reinforced during prior training. When the two disagree under semantic ambiguity, the prior usually wins: a methodology rule like "do not pitch before establishing Cost of Inaction" loses to the much stronger trained pattern of responding to a product question with a product answer. NEPQ (Acuff and Miner, 2023) requires a strict staged progressions:Connecting Situational Awareness Future Pace “Cost Of Inaction” (COI) Present Solution. Order is essential – if a salesperson tries to establish the “Pitch” before “COI”, the methodology fails. Generative models naturally collapse these phases due to their conversational priors. Finally, real dialogue is disorderly: users are sarcastic, contradict themselves, soften disagreement with politeness and shift tone without warning. Schegloff (1992) characterises competent dialogue as one in which participants actively repair such breakdowns. LLMs without explicit repair scaffolding default to literal interpretation, miss the affective signal and let the conversation drift off–methodology with no mechanism to re–anchor it to a structured conversation.The combined pressures of the above causes leads to the premature behavioural collapse. This design problem is therefore not just around simple dialogue generation, but regarding the effective enforcement of a non–negotiable, structured sequence against a model whose priors actively oppose it. Section 2.4 takes up this problem.1.4 Sales Structure FoundationThe system bifurcates sales interactions into Transactional or Consultative strategies based on product complexity and the degree to which buyer needs are already formed. The Transactional model suits straightforward purchases where requirements are explicit, prioritising efficiency and specification over discovery. The Consultative model applies where challenges are not yet fully articulated and the value of a solution carries inherent uncertainty, requiring deeper exploration before any offering is made.For consultative selling, the NEPQ framework (Acuff & Miner, 2023) was selected because its staged questioning sequence provides a transferable structure for sales training. Additionally, NEPQ operates consistently with established behavioural principles: its emphasis on self–generated reasoning aligns with motivational interviewing, where eliciting self–discovered insight is prioritised over directive instruction (Miller and Rollnick, 2012) and its focus on consequence–framing reflects loss aversion, whereby potential losses carry greater psychological weight than equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). BANT and MEDDIC were also considered but rejected as neither prescribes Socratic questioning nor psychological stage sequencing, making deterministic enforcement challenging.Full framework details are provided in the NEPQ Appendix.1.5 Requirements DefinitionThe solution had to be entirely cost–free. Given the scale and budget constraints of the final project, this was non–negotiable from the outset.A core functionality to include was a role–play system that works both ways. Users can play the prospect to understand the buyer's perspective, or step into the salesperson's shoes for live practise. Here, the AI should be programmed with different personality types to simulate the varying scenarios salespeople face in the real world.These interactions had to occur in real time. Any latency or delay would break the natural conversational flow, undermining the practise value entirely.An analysis component for these roleplays with beyond surface–level feedback actually allows salespeople to identify non–conducive habits they may be oblivious. Building on the existing model, a performance tracking component done continuously across individual profiles captures metrics at different stages of the conversation to deliver targeted feedback rather than generic observations so salespeople can work on highest leverage actions.Finally, a personal analytics layer is beneficial: a way to track individual progress over time, giving each user a clear picture of where they stand and how they are developing across sessions.The full requirements table is provided in Appendix A.Figure 2: Use Case Diagram – scope boundary and actor interactions for the Sales Roleplay Chatbot system2. Architectural Design2.1 Development Strategy & Architectural EvolutionPrototyping Strategy and RationaleAfter understanding the market positioning, an Extreme Programming (XP) approach was adopted to mitigate initial architectural uncertainty. Generative AI was utilised to assemble initial skeletons, allowing for refactoring and immediate empirical testing of the NEPQ framework. While this enabled rapid conceptual validation, early iterations produced a high degree of architectural coupling, serving merely as inspiration.Iteration 1: Local Model FailureThe initial strategy of a local Qwen 2.5–1.5B model had two fatal categories of failure. First, hardware constraints below a 6GB VRAM threshold imposed an unfeasible 300–second latency. Second, the model demonstrated insufficient neural capacity to resolve multi–constraint instructions. This manifested as “instruction bleed” (defined by ElevenLabs, 2024), where the model failed to distinguish between system–level methodology and conversational inputs. The ability to manage these competing priorities is strictly limited by parameter count (Kaplan et al., 2020). Consequently, the system consistently abandoned its persona or ignored the sales framework. Due to these compounding limitations in hardware and cognitive stability, the localized model was discarded for a deterministic cloud architecture.Iteration 2: Keyword–Driven MatchingTo eliminate Phase 1's per–turn LLM latency, the system switched to keyword matching for stage advancement. Responses became instant, but the logic broke.The problem: keyword presence was treated as confirmed intent. A single word like "want" anywhere in a message immediately set desired_outcome. One bot acknowledgment ("Got it") then satisfied both conditions in the advancement check, pushing INTENT → LOGICAL in just two turns (before budget, urgency, or decision authority had been established).# chatbot.py (initial iteration) – extraction and advancement crammed in one classif any(word in user_lower for word in ["need", "want", "looking for", "help with"]): self.extracted["desired_outcome"] = user_message[:100]def should_advance_stage(self, bot_response): has_signal = any(sig in bot_response.lower() for sig in ["got it", "makes sense"]) has_data = self.extracted["desired_outcome"] is not None return has_signal and has_data # Two turns → advanceThis violated NEPQ's core requirement: each stage must probe and confirm understanding across multiple turns before progressing. The failure cascaded: the LLM was prompted as though a genuine need had been surfaced, when in reality none had been explored. February’s implementation of a declarative FSM, config extraction and higher cohesion functions was the step in the right direction for an overall structural fix.2.2 Design Options – Why a FSM?Having eliminated approaches from Section 2.1, four further architectural options were evaluated and discarded before committing to the final design:Option A – Pure LLM (prompt–only, no control structure)The simplest approach: instruct the LLM via a long system prompt to follow the NEPQ framework. This was prototyped in the first week and discarded immediately because without hard guards, it experiences significant drift. (Section 1.4 for “drift”)Option B – LangChain AgentIts Reasoning and Acting (ReAct) loop uses probabilistic routing, prioritising semantic relevance over structural constraints, so any mention of pricing or features is likely to trigger the "Pitch" tool and bypass the mandatory Logical and Emotional stages. Because stage transitions happen inside the model's inference rather than in explicit code, the execution path cannot be enforced or verified, making defects difficult to isolate.Option C: Fine–Tuning Training a custom model on labelled sales conversations was considered but discarded due to high computational resource requirements and time constraints. Furthermore, there was the necessity of trial–and–error retraining for optimisations in which the scope of this project did not prove as the best use of resources as there are alternatives of existing models. Also, there was no sufficiently labelled dataset found during extensive research for NEPQ–specific conversations and creating such would not be the appropriate usage of time too.Option D: Keep external LLM provider but use strategy pattern The Strategy pattern failure is the most intellectually interesting rejection because it initially looks correct. The explanation makes two precise arguments: (1) strategies cannot be stateless across NEPQ phases without reintroducing the coupling they were meant to eliminate; and (2) the pattern controls what each stage does but cannot control when transitions happen, that is precisely a state machine's job. On its own, it can hold the sequence but not the quality of what is generated within each stage. No single mechanism closes every gap, so the architecture deploys three independent layers to catch what each previous one misses.LayerWhat it doesWhere it falls shortPrompt constraintsShape model behaviour by binding it to a defined role and stage before generation.Probabilistic, not enforced; instructions weaken under ambiguity or long context.FSM controlEnforce stage order; block transitions until a valid signal is observed.Operates on predefined signals only; cannot judge semantic intent.Output contractsValidate response structure after generation; reject distorted output before it reaches the user.Acts post–hoc; cannot prevent flawed internal reasoning, only its surfacing.The layers are deliberately independent, so failure in one does not cascade into the next. A prompt that drifts mid–generation cannot disrupt stage progression, because the FSM holds the current stage until a valid signal is confirmed; and a structurally invalid response is intercepted by the output contract before the user sees it. Even before drift can happen, the prompt rules are designed to prevent it anyway.2.5 Voice ModeFor the Voice Mode features, the core design principle is modality equivalence: voice and typed input converge into the same text–based chatbot pipeline, ensuring consistent follow–on actions (state tracking, response generation) regardless of input method.Most important design choice was creating the clear separation of concerns. This is a single–instance academic tool with no persistent user store and no financial data. This Section explains how the system uses abstraction to prevent dependency on a single provider and to guarantee continuous operation during API outages.The Problem: Single–Provider Dependency #strengthen slightlyInitially, the chatbot was connected to just one API provider (Groq) and experienced service interruptions. Groq's free–tier rate–limits were hit over 3 conversations in manual testing to explicitly flag the issue. Both times Groq's rate limits were hit during the project, SambaNova handled the request transparently – no manual intervention was needed and the user saw no interruption. Additionally, the Groq provider itself rotates between two API keys (SAFE_GROQ_API_KEY and ALTERNATIVE_GROQ_API_KEY) on rate–limit errors before escalating to the cross–provider fallback.Moreover, this accommodates future extensibility: additional providers or LLM modules can be integrated by implementing the existing interface, requiring no extensive refactoring of the core chatbot logic. The core design challenge this creates is consistency as an unconstrained LLM will improvise a buyer persona differently each turn, breaking the simulation's internal logic. The solution is to bind all mutable buyer state to a Python dataclass and rebuild the LLM's system prompt from that state on every turn, keeping the LLM's behaviour anchored to the same persona throughout the session.On initialisation, ProspectSession selects a persona using select_persona(), which draws randomly from a product–specific pool defined in prospect_config.yaml (covering domains such as luxury cars, b2b_saas, fitness, insurance and others), falling back to general–purpose personas when the product type is unrecognised. Automated suites ran continuously during development to catch regressions and lock behaviour contracts across all system layers. For example, test_ack_logic.py::test_terse_follow_up_after_validation_skips_repeat_ack caught subtle dialogue edge cases, while test_flow_hardening.py::test_intent_strategy_switches_to_transactional_for_budget_signal ensured FSM strategy switching logic was still intact even after changes made. These fast, repeatable checks prevented basic errors from persisting. Simultaneously, developers ran manual scenarios using test_detailed.py (a smoke–test harness) to observe real conversation flows and surface FSM issues automated scripts cannot detect–stage advancement sequences, natural dialogue progression and objection handling across multi–turn exchanges. The model ended replies with deferential prompts like "Would you like to hear more?" – an RLHF politeness artefact (see Section 1.4) that undermines sales authority before value is established. The fix applied three layers: a soft prohibition in prompts.py:_SHARED_RULES, an FSM guard against premature pitch context and a regex post–processor as a final safety net.Issue 2 – False stage advancement on weak signals. analysis_config.yaml initially contained fragments such as "want to", "need to" and "trying" – phrases too vague to carry intent, causing the FSM to advance from Intent to Logical after a single turn, violating the NEPQ requirement to confirm intent through situational questioning first. The fix removed weak phrases, applied the word–boundary technique described in Section 3.1.3 to prevent substring matches and added the negation window detailed in Section 3.1.3 for phrases like "I'm not struggling." Vague conversations now remain in Intent for three to four turns before advancing.Issue 3 – Over–probing and the interrogation effect. Without a question limit, the model produced two to three questions per turn in the Logical stage; testers found this adversarial. Per Conversational Repair theory, rapid–fire questioning constitutes a turn–taking violation that provokes defensiveness (see Section 1.4). The fix added a "BE HUMAN" rule to _SHARED_RULES: one declarative observation must precede any question, with a hard cap of one question per reply.Issue 4 – Premature pitching at Logical and Emotional stages. Product–related keywords caused the model to pitch features before emotional stakes were established, bypassing NEPQ's Consequence of Inaction step (Miner, 2022). Remote Microsoft Teams sessions reduces “Hawthorne effect” (McCambridge, Witton and Elbourne, 2014) because participants feel more comfortable in their own environments. Post–scenario interviews replace think–aloud protocol to prevent cognitive load that could affect natural task behaviour. Two fixed scenarios establish controlled conditions for comparable data and the third open–ended scenario deliberately surfaces edge–case behaviour that scripted prompts cannot expose. A deliberate split between sales–experienced and non–sales participants counters practitioner normalisation bias –experienced reps instinctively compensate for system gaps, while non–sales users reveal genuine usability and realism friction points. And having salespeople testing it makes sense as they will be actually using it. By having both, comparison can be done on such. Sample size of 10 used but with 4–6 used from each sales/non–sales background group, where this size is grounded in Nielsen's Law (Nielsen and Landauer, 1993) – ~85% of usability issues surface by N=5 with diminishing returns beyond. 4.3.2 AnalysisIn Section 4.3.2, a few figures are presented first. LLM Output VarianceLanguage models are probabilistic. Identical prompts yield different responses across runs. This is not a bug–it is the architecture. Consequence: The chatbot cannot guarantee identical behaviour in every situation, but staged constraints (YAML advancement rules, override checks) reduce variance to acceptable levels for training use.Empirical evidence: Across 170 domain tests with fixed prompts, completion rate variance ≈ ±2% turn–to–turn. Advancement stage remains consistent 94% of the time due to deterministic FSM rules.2. Context Window LimitationsThe LLM operates on a finite context window (~4k tokens per conversation). Extended conversations compress older context, potentially losing earlier problem statements. Mitigation: Session summaries are injected before each API call to maintain problem continuity.Addressable Limitations (Deliberate Scope Choices)LimitationImpactWhy Not FixedFor ProductionSession persistenceSessions stored in memory; lost on server restartTrade–off: File–based persistence adds latency (~50ms per write); in–memory is acceptable for training toolRequires database (PostgreSQL) or RedisInput validationPattern–matching filters only; regex does not catch all injection attemptsSufficient for this scope (non–adversarial, controlled trainer input); not suitable for public APIsRequires token–based input sanitisation + WAFSingle–server deploymentNo horizontal scaling; max ~50 concurrent sessions before degradationArchitectural choice: Flask app is stateless; sessions in–memory breaks distributed designRequires microservices + session storeProvider failure handlingNo retry logic; Groq timeout → immediate API error returned to userGroq uptime 99.8% (audited); retries add 2–3s per failure; not justified for training contextImplement exponential backoff + fallback provider queueIntent classificationAmbiguous phrasings (e.g., "maybe later" vs. "I need to think") remain misclassifiedWould require LLM–based classifier (adds 300ms latency per turn); keyword heuristic is 85% accurate for clear intentAcceptable for training; retrain classifier for productionKnown Gaps From Testing CoverageEdge cases in objection reframing (6/170 tests involved manual expectation–setting, not automated assertions)Quiz scoring fallback (LLM failure → score 50); no keyword–based fallback implementedProspect mode scoring (deterministic keyword–based scoring; no A/B ground truth from real salespeople)Inherent LLM constraints (variance, context limits) are well–understood and mitigated where possible. Hartmann et al. (2023) confirm that the intersection of sales and ethics is "vast and, therefore, difficult to summarize" spanning over four decades of unresolved scholarly debate – precisely because the same techniques that build trust can equally exploit it.The individual salesperson's moral judgment sits at the centre of this debate. Ingram et al. (2007) argue that “enhancing the moral behaviour of salespeople is increasingly important, but difficult " given that sales management decisions directly shape frontline ethical conduct.The system is deployed as a supplementary practice tool, designed to reward consultative behaviours – specifically active listening, open–ended questioning and empathy, rather than high–pressure or directive interaction patterns. Stages cannot advance without customer–signal detection, structurally incentivising discovery over persuasion.4.7 Future workVoice mode would benefit most from paralinguistic analysis: real–time detection of filler words, talk–to–listen ratio and pacing against stage norms would surface coaching signals unavailable in text–only sessions, extending the framework’s feedback depth into delivery as well as content.Performance persistence is a further priority: persisting analytics per user profile across sessions would enable the system to surface each practitioner’s highest–leverage improvement areas and track longitudinal progress, converting one–off practice into a structured development pathway.A sales call analysis feature, enabling users to upload or record real calls for automated framework–aligned feedback, would extend the system’s value beyond simulated practice into live performance review.A gamification layer would introduce XP awarded per session scaled by performance score, unlocking difficulty tiers and prospect variants as practitioners improve, with a team leaderboard surfacing rankings by weekly XP and stage progression to introduce the social accountability and motivation of an in–person sales floor. Crucially, all required inputs already exist in the deterministic scoring pipeline (score_session()), meaning implementation requires only a persistence layer and lightweight UI–making this among the highest–ROI extensions for a future iteration.4.8 Personal ReflectionI front–loaded too much time on designing the initial architecture before any implementation had been tested. Given that I naturally work through trial and error and manual testing, this was a poor fit for how I actually think. A tighter design–implement cycle from the start would have moved the project forward faster and grounded early decisions in real evidence rather than untested assumptions. A tighter build–test–redesign loop would have served the project better from the outset.If I could revisit any technical decision, it would be the move from keyword–matching to a cloud API provider. It would have been interesting to push further with keyword–matching (as this would be far more deterministic), augmenting it with NLP techniques for tighter framework adherence and introduced the LLM later purely as a corrective layer to pull conversations back on–track when they drifted or edge–cases. And in prospect–mode, LLM used only to create variance and unique feel. Personally, I have matured significantly. Investing heavily in prompt engineering, I developed a durable skill in controlled, structured and academically–backed prompt engineering while rooting myself in applying behavioural psychology. Given the rapid development of the AI industry, this is a skill I will carry forward.5. References Acuff, J. and Miner, J. (2023) The New Model of Selling: Selling to an Unsellable Generation.
