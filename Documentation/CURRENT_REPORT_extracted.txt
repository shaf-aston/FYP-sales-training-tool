ABSTRACT
Objective: Sales training is effective but expensive and difficult to scale. Online alternatives lack live conversational practice. This project builds an AI chatbot that simulates a prospect in a structured sales conversation, enabling salespeople to practise on demand.
Methods: Tried local LLM usage, keyword-matching system and finally transitioned into a FSM design.
Measured outcomes: The architecture achieved a 92% stage adherence accuracy over 25 developer-tested scenarios and an 84.5 System Usability Scale (SUS) score from independent UAT participants. The resulting system comprises of {~6,600 LOC of Python/HTML/JS plus ~1,050 lines of YAML configuration}, deployed at zero cost on Render's free tier.
Conclusions: The architecture proves that FSM-enforced stage gating combined with LLM generation can reliably train methodology adherence without fine-tuning or expensive proprietary models.

Problem Identification:
The global corporate training market is valued at approximately $345 billion USD (Grand View Research, 2023). Three specific inefficiencies in the sales training segment stood out:
Cost: For a 10-person SME team, a single round of individual roleplay sessions costs £3,000–£5,000. That's the budget for practice alone, not training content. 1.2 Technical Exploration & Previous Approaches (300)
Hardware constraints (development machine):
Phase 1: Built a local chatbot prototype using Qwen 2.5-1.5B model locally hosted, simulating a fitness coaching client for sales rep practice. This failed in several dimensions: 
1. Hardware Constraints & Inference Degradation:  The 4GB VRAM machine fell short of the 6-8GB requirement of this 1.5B parameter inference, resulting in 5-minute response times per turn. Background threading and context truncation as mitigations to lighten the load failed as hardware-bound bottlenecks reached their physical limit, rendering it practically unpleasant
2. Task Complexity
Research confirms that instruction-following fidelity scales with model size; sub-3B parameter models consistently struggle under multi-constraint loads. Attempting to control the 1.5B model through granular constraint prompting backfired, triggering “instruction bleed” (defined by ElevenLabs.io). This led to “attention dilution” and fragmented execution: the model prioritized instructions erratically, resulting in NEPQ non-adherence and role instability; consequently, the model frequently abandoned its prospect persona to adopt a generic AI assistant tone or the role of the sales representative.
These architectural constraints were further exacerbated by implementation gaps in the inference protocol. Without explicit End-Of-Sequence (EOS) token handling, the model lacked a "stop signal," causing it to hallucinate dialogue or truncate mid-sentence. Additionally, the use of unprocessed JSON datasets introduced syntax interference; the model’s attention was diverted by technical formatting (such as brackets and keys), which overrode system prompts and broke the logical flow of the sales conversation

3. Strategic Deprecation of Speech-to-Text (STT)
A voice-first pipeline utilizing OpenAI’s Whisper was evaluated to maximize roleplay realism. However, it was deliberately excluded from the current scope because:
Risk Compounding: Phase 1 identified latency and context loss as critical failure modes. Integrating real-time audio streaming and Whisper inference would have introduced multiple new integration points, creating an unacceptable risk of total system regression.
Latency Budget Overages: The cumulative delay of a full voice loop (transcription → LLM inference → TTS → playback) far exceeded the threshold for realistic roleplay, making the tool ineffective as a real-time training benchmark.
Phase 2 transitioned to a deterministic keyword-based architecture, successfully resolving the latency and sequencing failures of Phase 1. However it arrived with its own limitations:
Contextual Flattening: The "label-then-lookup" process acts as a filter that strips away detail. Once the system classifies a user’s input into a category (the label), it discards the original sentence. This "information loss" means the bot cannot see the difference between two different ways of saying the same thing, forcing it to deliver a generic, one-size-fits-all response that ignores the unique "feel" of the conversation.
Uni-dimensional Tone Mapping: The system lacked a dedicated output channel for tone. Because the architecture relied on a 1:1 mapping (single intent label to a single fixed string), it could not modulate its delivery based on the user's emotional state or the required sales persona.
Lexical Brittleness: The detection logic relied on surface-form keyword matching rather than semantic intent. This caused frequent "missed advances" or incorrect transitions; if a user used varied vocabulary to convey a known intent (e.g., an affordability objection), the system failed to recognize the meaning because it was measuring word-proximity rather than intent.
Monolithic Strategy Coupling: The lack of an abstraction layer between pattern matching and response selection prevented flexible execution. For example, an affordability objection requires different handling depending on the sales strategy; without this layer, the entire logic structure had to be duplicated for every new flow, making maintenance unsustainable.
During developer testing across both above phases, drift was the most consistent failure mode observed:
Phase 3 (hybrid architecture: Strategy Pattern was initially used for conversation control but was replaced by an FSM as it created fragmentation and coupling that made every feature change require tracing multiple files. This exposed the core problem: “hallucinated stage adherence”, where the bot advanced to later stages (e.g. pitch) before the user had met the prerequisites. 
Solution – FSM controls when to advance, LLM controls what to say.

Domain Risk: The AI Training Paradox
The core risk of an unstructured LLM for sales training is not that it fails to teach — it is that it actively teaches the wrong behaviour. A bot that skips discovery, jumps to pricing before establishing pain, or folds on objections without probing trains reps to do the same. The failure pattern gets reinforced, not corrected.
This is qualitatively different from a product quality failure. An AI trainer that consistently models the wrong sequence creates a direct, measurable revenue risk when those sales representatives face real prospects. Conversational fluency alone is not sufficient here, which is precisely why the FSM constraint layer exists: to enforce stage order deterministically and block advancement until specific user signals are detected. Fluency without methodology enforcement is a fluency simulator, not a training tool.

2. CURRENT PROJECT 
2.1 Architecture & Design
2.1.1 Why FSM Over Strategy Pattern
Problem-pattern fit: The Strategy Pattern solves algorithm selection at runtime (e.g., choosing between sorting algorithms). The problem here was sequential state flow where transitions depend on explicit user signals, not dynamic algorithm dispatch. Using Strategy Pattern for what is fundamentally a state machine problem created structural mismatch that manifested as maintenance burden and edge-case bugs.
Hence, birth was given to the FSM.
2.2.2 Drift Prevention as Design Principle
The primary design objective was to prevent uncontrolled stage transitions. In the final architecture:
Only FSM guards can transition stage; LLM output is constrained to language generation within the active stage.
Transitions depend on explicit user signals (detected via NLU pipeline), not turn counts or LLM-inferred intent.
Stage prompts include P1/P2/P3 constraint hierarchies that override RLHF politeness defaults.
This separation means a behaviourally drifted LLM response cannot derail methodology progression as the FSM holds stage until valid advancement signals are detected.
2.1.3 Consultative State Model
The FSM enforces the five-stage NEPQ consultative flow. Each transition has explicit guard conditions that prevent LLM-driven stage skipping:

SRP refactoring extracted training, knowledge, and analysis responsibilities from orchestration logic, reducing coupling and simplifying testing boundaries. 2.2 Iterative Development & Prompt Engineering
2.2.1 The Iteration Method
Drift was not solved in one pass. The final system is the result of multiple observe-hypothesise-fix-validate cycles. Each row in the table below represents a complete iteration cycle, not a theoretical improvement:
Measurement Methodology for Iteration Metrics: Stage accuracy was calculated as (correct FSM transitions / total observed transitions) across 25 developer-executed test scenarios. Each scenario was run once with the same Groq model configuration and YAML product settings. "Correct" was defined as: (a) stage advanced only when advancement rules in _check_advancement_condition() were satisfied, and (b) no stage was skipped without meeting prerequisite signals. Objection resolution rate was measured as (appropriately reframed responses / total objection turns) using manual developer assessment against the CLASSIFY→RECALL→REFRAME scaffold. All baseline metrics were captured before the fix; achieved metrics were captured after the fix, using the same scenario set to ensure comparability.
2.3.2 Why Layered Control, Not Just Better Prompts
A representative fix was the small-talk loop. Initial attempts added bridging heuristics that made conversations more rigid. The final fix removed most of that logic and replaced it with simpler prompt constraints and progression rules.
Across iterations, the dominant failure class was drift: outputs that were fluent but misaligned with the required stage intent. A key insight emerged: prompts alone cannot fully constrain LLM behaviour. The model's RLHF training creates strong priors (e.g., ending statements with questions to seem helpful) that override explicit prompt instructions in edge cases.
The solution was layered control:
Prompt constraints (soft): Guide the model's generation direction
FSM guards (hard): Block stage transitions until user signals are detected
Output contracts (hard): Regex-based post-processing catches remaining violations
This three-layer approach achieved near-complete drift containment (Section 4.1) whereas prompt-only attempts plateaued at ~70% success.
Iteration method used throughout: observe failure, form hypothesis, apply minimal change, validate across scenario set, and retain only changes that improved measured behaviour.
Code examples for these iterations are provided in Appendix A.
2.4 Implementation Details
The provider abstraction prevented architecture lock-in when API limits were hit mid-development. Instead of rewriting orchestration, providers were switched via factory configuration.
Before abstraction, provider changes required repeated call-site edits. After abstraction, provider switching became a configuration concern.
Current design additions integrated into the same architecture:
Trainer panel and coaching assistant flows share the same analysis and state context so coaching does not bypass drift controls.
Quiz module reuses stage semantics to test methodology understanding using the same directional model as the live chat.
Rewind/replay tooling supports maintenance and verification by allowing deterministic re-evaluation of turns after edits.

2.6.3 Key Estimation Insight
Prompt engineering consumed 31% of total effort but was initially estimated at ~14% (~10h). The 120% overrun on this component was the project's only significant estimation failure.
Why the estimate failed: The initial estimate used analogical reasoning, comparing prompt tuning to code debugging. Both involve observing unexpected behaviour and making targeted changes. However, the analogy breaks down:
This analogy failure is a transferable lesson: prompt engineering effort cannot be estimated by comparing to traditional development tasks. It behaves more like experimental research, where each hypothesis requires a full test cycle.
Corrective action: Future prompt-heavy LLM projects should estimate prompt tuning at 2-3× initial expectation unless the team has prior experience with the specific model and domain.

4.2.3 Evaluation Rigour & Developer Bias Mitigation
Initial evaluations were heavily weighted by developer-centric testing, inherently introducing bias as the developer unconsciously navigates known edge cases. To introduce rigorous methodological validity, this bias was actively mitigated through the execution of independent User Acceptance Testing (UAT, Section 3.2.3) with 8 participants blind to the underlying FSM architecture. 

While the UAT confirmed system usability (SUS: 84.5), the evaluation constraints must be acknowledged clearly. Due to the limited sample size (n=8), confidence intervals are wide, meaning the generalizability of quantitative metrics (like the 92% stage accuracy observed in developer testing) cannot be statistically guaranteed for populations of thousands of users. Furthermore, qualitative outcomes were derived from simulated roleplay environments, and while effective for proxy measurement, they remain bounded by the lack of true financial stakes found in actual sales calls. Consequently, while the evaluation is methodical and evidence-based, claims of efficacy are restricted to the context of training enablement.

5. Reflection
5.1 Personal Reflection
The most important shift was from "using recommended patterns" to "measuring whether a pattern fits the problem."
I initially valued architecture style over outcome clarity. The Strategy Pattern implementation looked academically sophisticated, but its maintenance burden was measurable: 45 minutes per feature review, fragmented logic across 5 files, and shared imports creating hidden coupling. The FSM refactor was not an aesthetic preference — it was a response to measured costs.
This mindset shift is transferable. In future projects, I will ask "what does this design cost to maintain?" rather than "does this design look correct in a textbook?"
5.2 Key Lessons
Lesson 1: The first fix is usually incomplete.
Early drift fixes addressed individual symptoms (permission questions, tone mismatches) without understanding the common cause. The insight that these were all instances of drift enabled a unified solution: layered control. Resist the temptation to close tickets with symptom-level patches.
Lesson 2: Prompt engineering is not a hack.
Prompt engineering consumed 31% of total effort. This was not wasted time — it was the core mechanism by which drift was controlled. Treating prompt tuning as a "quick fix" rather than disciplined engineering caused the only significant schedule overrun. In projects involving behavioural LLM control, prompt engineering should be planned like experimental research, not like code debugging.
Lesson 3: Pivot decisions should be based on measured cost, not emotional cost.
The architectural pivot felt like failure initially. However, the measured outcome was: 50% LOC reduction, 78% review time reduction, and zero coupling-related bugs post-refactor. The sunk cost of the original implementation was far smaller than the ongoing cost of maintaining a poor fit. Future projects should have explicit "stop and measure" checkpoints before committing to significant refactors.
5.3 Future Work
5.4 Research Directions
The project raises several questions worth systematic investigation:
1. Comparative methodology study: Does NEPQ-constrained prompting outperform SPIN, BANT, or unconstrained baselines under identical evaluation conditions?
2. Prompt-constraint optimisation: What is the relationship between constraint specificity, constraint length, and LLM compliance? Are shorter constraints more or less effective than detailed instructions?
3. Adversarial reliability: How well do layered controls hold under deliberately adversarial inputs designed to trigger drift? What is the minimum control stack required for acceptable reliability?
These questions are empirically testable with the current architecture and would contribute to broader understanding of LLM behaviour control.
